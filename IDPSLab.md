# Cisco Adaptive Security Appliance (ASA) Lab
The 2023 Cloudflare DDoS Attack<br>
Introduction
Distributed Denial-of-Service (DDoS) attacks have become a major threat to Internet stability and organizational security. These attacks aim to overwhelm servers or networks with excessive requests, rendering services inaccessible to legitimate users. In 2023, Cloudflare, a leading web performance and security company, faced one of the largest and most sophisticated DDoS campaigns ever recorded. This report argues that the 2023 Cloudflare attack demonstrates how advanced DDoS operations exploit Internet protocols to maximize disruption and that only automated, large-scale, and collaborative defense systems can effectively mitigate such threats.
Why the Attack
The motivation behind large-scale DDoS campaigns typically includes political activism, financial extortion, or attempts to test the resilience of high-profile networks. In Cloudflare’s case, attackers appeared to target major online platforms and infrastructure providers supported by Cloudflare, aiming to disrupt services and demonstrate the power of their botnets. These attacks were not directed solely at Cloudflare but at the company’s clients, using Cloudflare’s network as a defensive battleground.
Threat Actor (Attacker)
While Cloudflare did not attribute the attack to a specific threat actor, reports suggest that it originated from globally distributed botnets composed of compromised Internet of Things (IoT) devices and cloud servers. Such botnets are often sold or rented on underground cybercrime markets, enabling various hacker groups to launch coordinated attacks without possessing direct technical expertise.
Timeline and Frequency
The first major 2023 incident occurred in February, when Cloudflare mitigated an unprecedented 71 million requests per second (RPS) attack, setting a new record for the largest DDoS event in history. Throughout the year, Cloudflare observed multiple record breaking waves. In its third quarter 2023 DDoS Threat Report, the company revealed that several subsequent attacks exceeded 100 million RPS, marking a continuing escalation. By the end of 2023, Cloudflare’s systems had automatically mitigated millions of DDoS events, ranging from small scale floods to hyper-volumetric campaigns. This persistence indicates that DDoS operations have evolved into continuous, automated assaults rather than isolated incidents.



Vulnerability 
The most notable vulnerability exploited in the 2023 attack was related to the HTTP/2 protocol, a standard used by most modern websites to optimize web traffic. Attackers abused a flaw in the way HTTP/2 handled rapid stream resets, a method later termed the “Rapid Reset” attack. By quickly opening and cancelling multiple streams, attackers could send massive volumes of requests while consuming minimal resources on their end.
This vulnerability allowed adversaries to achieve amplification without using reflection services traditionally exploited in DDoS attacks. The result was a new generation of application layer floods that were both powerful and efficient. Additionally, the continued presence of unsecured IoT devices provided attackers with a vast network of endpoints to launch simultaneous attacks.
Mitigation and Defense
Cloudflare’s defense strategy in 2023 relied on automation, distributed infrastructure, and protocol specific countermeasures. Its global edge network, spanning over 270 cities, allowed it to absorb and filter malicious traffic close to its source, minimizing latency and preventing network congestion. Cloudflare’s autonomous mitigation system detected anomalies in real time and applied countermeasures within seconds, ensuring uninterrupted service for legitimate users.
When the HTTP/2 “Rapid Reset” vulnerability was discovered, Cloudflare quickly collaborated with other major service providers, including Google and Amazon, to develop and deploy patches and mitigation techniques. These included rate limiting, request validation, and temporary restrictions on specific protocol behaviors. Cloudflare’s Web Application Firewall (WAF) and machine learning algorithms further adapted to detect abnormal traffic patterns and block malicious requests without manual intervention.
The company’s transparency in publicly sharing findings through its quarterly DDoS Threat Reports also contributed to industry wide awareness. This collective effort demonstrates that effective DDoS defense extends beyond one company’s capacity and depends on coordinated global collaboration.
Conclusion	
The 2023 Cloudflare DDoS attacks underscore the growing sophistication and persistence of modern denial-of-service campaigns. These attacks exploited weaknesses in widely used Internet protocols, proving that even robust cloud-based infrastructures are not immune to evolving threats. Cloudflare’s rapid and automated response showcased the critical importance of scalable defense mechanisms, real-time detection, and industry collaboration.
This incident matters to network security because it reveals that future resilience depends on continuous innovation, automated threat intelligence sharing, and secure protocol design. As cybercriminals continue to weaponize everyday technology for large-scale disruptions, proactive defense and global cooperation will remain essential to safeguarding the reliability and security of the Internet.
